# Copy Reference — Headlines, CTAs & Microcopy

Design without copy is decoration. On a landing page, every word is either earning
its place or taking up space. This reference covers how to write placeholder copy
that fits the design system — not Lorem Ipsum, but real words that demonstrate
how the page should sound and what it should say.

Use this reference whenever writing or evaluating copy for an artifact, especially
when the user hasn't provided their own content.

---

## The Hierarchy of Landing Page Copy

Every landing page communicates in a stack. From most-read to least-read:

1. **The headline** — read by almost everyone
2. **The subtext** — read by people the headline didn't lose
3. **Section headings** — scanned by scrollers looking for relevance
4. **Card titles and bullet points** — read if the section heading matched
5. **Body paragraphs** — read by the genuinely interested
6. **Footer copy** — read by almost no one, but still must be correct

Write the most important levels first, and write them best. A mediocre headline
with a great body paragraph is a failed landing page. A great headline with a
mediocre body paragraph still converts.

---

## Headline Formulas

A headline should be specific, confident, and about the reader — not the product.
"We built X" is a product headline. "Do X without Y" is a reader headline.

**The outcome headline:**
State the result the user gets, not the feature that delivers it.
Format: [Achieve outcome] [without / in / for] [qualifier]
Examples: "Ship faster without breaking things." / "A portfolio that gets you
hired." / "Analytics for teams that move quickly."

**The contrast headline:**
Name a frustration, then offer the alternative.
Format: [The old way] is [problem]. [This] is [better way].
Examples: "Spreadsheets weren't built for this. We were." / "Generic templates,
or something actually yours. We think you know."

**The direct value headline:**
Plain, declarative, no clever framing. Works when the value is genuinely strong
enough to not need dressing up.
Examples: "The fastest way to build a portfolio." / "Everything your team needs
to ship." / "Your work, beautifully presented."

**The question headline:**
Opens a gap in the reader's mind that the page then closes. Use sparingly — a
question headline that doesn't get answered immediately feels like a bait.
Examples: "What if your tools actually helped you think?" / "Ready to stop
explaining your work and start showing it?"

**What to avoid:**
- Vague superlatives: "The best," "world-class," "next-generation" — these mean
  nothing without proof.
- Internal language: feature names, acronyms, or technical terms the reader may
  not know yet.
- We-first phrasing: "We are a platform for..." — start with the reader or
  the outcome, not the company.

## Subtext Rules

Subtext is the one paragraph that has to do three things: clarify the headline,
add one specific detail, and set up the CTA. It has two sentences, maybe three.

**Length:** 20–40 words. Longer than 40 words means it's doing too much work —
cut it or move content to a feature section.

**Tone:** One level warmer and plainer than the headline. If the headline is bold
and punchy, the subtext is calm and specific. They are a conversation, not a repetition.

**What to say:** The subtext answers "okay, but how?" or "okay, but who is this
for?" It adds the one qualifier or proof point that makes the headline credible.
Example: Headline "Ship faster without breaking things." → Subtext "A design
system built for teams who've outgrown their old toolkit. Works in React, HTML,
or whatever you're already using."

**What to never say in subtext:**
- Don't restate the headline in different words — that's just padding.
- Don't start listing features — that's what the feature section is for.
- Don't use "we" more than once. The subtext is about the reader.

## CTA Button Copy

The button label is the most-read copy on the page after the headline. Default
button labels ("Submit," "Click here," "Learn more") are invisible — the user's
eye slides past them because they carry no specific meaning.

**Good CTA patterns:**
- Verb + outcome: "Start building free" / "Get my portfolio" / "See how it works"
- Verb + object (specific): "View live projects" / "Download the template"
- Conversational invitation: "Let's talk" / "Show me more" / "Take a look"

**Avoid:**
- "Submit" — implies paperwork
- "Click here" — describes the interaction, not the value
- "Learn more" — where? More what? Too vague to act on
- "Get started" — acceptable but overused; try to be more specific when possible

**Primary vs secondary CTA copy:**
The primary CTA is the highest-commitment action (sign up, contact, buy). The
secondary CTA is the lowest-commitment action (see examples, view demo, read more).
They should feel like they're at different levels of investment — not equal.
Example: primary "Start for free" / secondary "See how it works" — the second
feels exploratory rather than committal.

## Eyebrow Labels (Section Openers)

The small uppercase text that appears above a headline or section title. Its job
is to provide context before the headline delivers the message.

**Rules:**
- Maximum 3 words. Usually 1–2.
- No punctuation except hyphens.
- Factual and specific, not clever: "What you get" not "The magic." / "How it
  works" not "The journey." / "Built for teams" not "For the visionaries."
- Uses the accent color — it is the one place in the type hierarchy where the
  accent appears in text form.

## Feature Card Copy

Each feature card has a title and a 2–3 sentence description. Both must earn
their place.

**The title:** A noun phrase, not a sentence. 2–4 words. Names the capability,
not the benefit. Examples: "Version control" / "Real-time preview" / "Export
anywhere." The benefit is what the description is for.

**The description:** Two sentences. Sentence one: what it does. Sentence two:
why that matters to the reader. Do not pack both into one long sentence — they
need to land separately.
Example title: "Real-time preview" → Description: "See your changes instantly
as you build. No refresh cycle, no guessing — what you see is what ships."

## Testimonial Copy

Testimonial quotes should be edited, not transcribed. Long real quotes become
unreadable in a card. The goal is one strong, specific sentence that could only
come from that person's experience.

**What makes a good testimonial quote:**
- Specific result, not vague praise: "Cut our onboarding from 3 days to half
  a day" not "Really great product."
- Sounds like a person talking, not a press release: "I was skeptical at first"
  is more credible than "This solution exceeded all expectations."
- Contains a before/after or emotional shift: "I used to dread client reviews.
  Now I look forward to them."

When writing placeholder testimonials, invent names, roles, and companies that
fit the product's likely customer profile. A SaaS tool for developers gets
testimonials from CTOs and engineering leads. A portfolio builder gets testimonials
from designers and freelancers.

## Footer & Legal Microcopy

**Copyright line:** "© [Year] [Name or Company]. All rights reserved." is the
standard. Never "Copyright © [Year]" — the © symbol already means copyright.

**Privacy / Terms links:** If the user has these pages, include them in the footer
as plain text links. If they don't, omit them entirely — a broken link to a
non-existent page is worse than no link.

**Newsletter placeholder:** If the footer includes an email input, the label should
be specific: "Get design tips weekly" not "Subscribe." The button: "Subscribe" is
acceptable here since context makes the action clear, but "Get the newsletter" or
"Join [X] readers" is better.

**Empty state messages (for interactive artifacts):** Be specific and helpful.
"No projects yet" is fine. "Add your first project to see it here" is better.
"It's quiet in here. Add a project to get started." is too cute — reserve warmth
for onboarding, not empty states that the user will see often.
